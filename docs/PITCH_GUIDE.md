## Part 0 — The one mental model

Everything else in this document unfolds from this picture. Hold it in
your head before you say a word.

**NeuraLaunch takes a person with a vague idea or a stalled situation
and walks them to a launched product.**

Structurally, it works like this:

- A user owns **Ventures** — long-lived things they're working on (1 on
  Execute tier, 3 on Compound).
- Each Venture runs as a sequence of **Cycles**.
- A Cycle has five stages, in order:
  1. **Interview** — Discovery
  2. **Recommendation** — one committed path, with a pushback loop
  3. **Roadmap** — week-by-week plan
  4. **Execution** — tasks, check-ins, and five tools
  5. **What's Next** — a diagnostic chat or a 5-section brief with forks
- At the end of the cycle, the founder picks a **fork**. The fork
  becomes the seed for the next cycle in the same venture.

Inside Execution, the five tools are: **Research**, **Conversation
Coach**, **Outreach Composer**, **Service Packager**, **Validation
Page**. Each can be launched from a specific task, or standalone.

That's the whole picture. Every pitch below is a zoom of this.

### 5-minute deep pitch (for investors, partners, advisors)

*(Own it — don't memorise word-for-word.)*

**Open with the pain.**

There are two kinds of people stuck on the same problem.

The first has a vague idea. They haven't started. They've talked to
ChatGPT for hours and gotten ten contradictory answers, because
generic AI doesn't know them. It doesn't know they have $400, not
$40,000. It doesn't know they have eight hours a week, not forty. It
doesn't know they live in Lagos, not San Francisco. Every plan it
gives them is for somebody else.

The second started and got stuck. They've tried a few things, nothing
worked, and now they don't know whether to keep going, pivot, or
walk. They don't have anyone who knows their full picture to argue
with honestly.

Both people have the same real problem: **they have no honest,
patient, grounded thinking partner who knows them end to end.**

**NeuraLaunch is that partner.**

The person opens what we call a **Venture** — one active thing on
Execute, three on Compound. Each venture runs as a sequence of
cycles, and each cycle has five stages.

**Stage one is the Interview.** A real conversation, powered by a
belief-state engine that tracks fifteen things about the person —
situation, goal, hours, money, market, skill, fear, why now. Every
answer updates a confidence score. The AI picks the next question by
information gain — whichever answer would teach the most. The
interview ends only when enough is known to make a grounded
recommendation.

**Stage two is the Recommendation.** A two-step pipeline — a fast
model summarises who they are, a fast model rules out what doesn't
fit, and then the big model, with live web research tools, writes
*one* committed path. Not three options. One. With real first steps
and honest risks and the alternatives that were considered and
rejected. Then the person reads it and either accepts it, or pushes
back. The pushback loop runs up to fifteen rounds on the top tier.
The AI reads whether each objection is analytical, fear, or lack of
belief, and it either defends, refines, or replaces the path. If they
push past the hard cap, we generate a sibling recommendation based on
the path they were arguing for — they can compare the two side by
side and pick their own.

**Stage three is the Roadmap.** Once they accept, a durable background
job builds a phased, week-by-week plan scoped to *their* real hours.
If they said six hours a week, no task takes more than six hours.

**Stage four is Execution.** They work tasks; they check in on them.
Each check-in goes through an AI agent that already knows their
recommendation, their roadmap, the specific task, and — if they used
any of the five tools on that task — exactly what they did. If they
prepped a sales call with the Coach and it ran yesterday, the agent
asks how the real call compared to the prep. The five tools are:
Research (live-web, 25 steps), Conversation Coach (prep + roleplay +
debrief), Outreach Composer (single, batch, or three-touch
sequence), Service Packager (named tiers with prices and a one-page
brief), and Validation Page (a real landing page, distribution
brief, and honest market read). Every tool can be called from a task
or launched standalone.

**Stage five is What's Next.** When they hit the "What's Next?" button,
we check how much of the roadmap they completed. If they did zero or
under 70%, they enter a diagnostic chat — the AI asks what went
wrong, what stalled, whether to pivot. If they did 70% or more, they
go straight to a five-section brief: what happened, what I got
wrong, what the evidence says, two or three forks with "right if"
conditions, and the parking lot of ideas they captured along the
way. They pick a fork. That becomes the seed for the next cycle in
the same venture.

**Across cycles, the product remembers them.** A founder profile
builds up automatically — their actual pace, their avoidance
patterns, their tool preferences, their strengths. The next roadmap
calibrates to their *real* hours, not the hours they once claimed.
If they said ten but actually averaged five, the next plan is built
around five, and we tell them we're doing that — no silent
correction.

**We make money on subscription.** Free gives a one-off recommendation,
two lifetime attempts. Execute is $29 a month — one active venture,
full roadmap, ten pushback rounds, all five tools. Compound is $49 —
three parallel ventures, fifteen pushback rounds, voice input on
mobile, priority.

**Why this works now.** Three years ago the models couldn't hold a
real conversation and return reliable structured output at the same
time. They can now. Durable execution (Inngest) means a
three-minute synthesis never "starts over" when a deploy happens.
The belief-state engine, the pushback loop, and the cross-cycle
memory — they compound on each other. Generic AI can't replicate
this because generic AI doesn't remember.

**One line to close:** most AI products give you answers. NeuraLaunch
walks with you, remembers what you did, and tells you the truth
about what happened.

## Part 2 — The product in plain words

**What it is.** A software product, on web and mobile, that helps a
single person take an idea from "vague" to "launched and tested with
real people" — and then keep going with a second idea, a third, a
pivot, whatever comes next.

**What it is not.**
- **Not** a code generator. It does not write your app for you.
- **Not** a generic AI chat.
- **Not** a course or a community.
- **Not** a no-code builder.
- **Not** a project management tool.
- **Not** a marketing automation platform.

**The magic word:** *grounded*. Every suggestion it makes is grounded in
what the person told us and what the person did. No generic advice.
One specific path, built on their real constraints.

**Who it's for.**
- Someone frozen with a vague idea who has never started.
- Someone mid-journey, stuck, deciding whether to push, pivot, or walk.
- A recent graduate who wants to build something but doesn't know how.
- A domain expert (teacher, lawyer, operator) who knows they have a
  valuable skill but can't figure out how to sell it.
- A technical founder who keeps building instead of selling.

**Who it's not for.**
- People who already have traction and just need scale.
- People looking for a course.
- People who want AI to write their code.

## Part 3 — The five stages of a cycle, in plain words

### Stage 1 — **Interview** (Discovery)

*"Before I tell you what to build, I need to know you."*

The person lands on a blank chat. They type their situation in their
own words. The AI reads it and, critically, extracts *everything* it
can learn in one shot — budget, market, skill, goal — in a single
call. Then it asks the next best question.

Under the hood, the AI keeps a running **belief state** — fifteen
fields about the person, each with a confidence score from 0 to 1.
The interview runs through four internal phases: Orientation (who are
you), Goal Clarity (what do you want), Constraint Map (what do you
have), and Conviction (how serious are you). The AI picks the next
question by information gain — whichever answer would teach the most.

Interview ends when the belief state is full enough to synthesise —
meaning overall confidence is at least 80% and no critical field is
still blank. Hard ceiling of 15 questions, no matter what.

**What the founder sees:** a streaming chat. Their message, then the
next question typed out in real time. A quiet phase indicator at the
top. When the AI is ready to recommend, the screen transitions to
synthesis.

### Stage 2 — **Recommendation** (with pushback)

*"Here is one path. Not three. Argue with me if you disagree."*

Synthesis runs a three-step pipeline. Fast model summarises the
person in 3–5 factual sentences. Fast model lists three possible
directions and explains why two don't fit. Big model (with live web
research) writes one committed recommendation.

The founder lands on a recommendation card with:

- A two-to-three-sentence summary
- A "what would make this wrong" paragraph — surfaced right next to
  the thesis, not hidden
- Your path, Why this fits you, First three steps, Time to first
  result, Risks & mitigations, Assumptions, Alternatives rejected

Three things can happen next:

**They accept.** One click — "This is my path, build my roadmap."
Background job fires; the roadmap starts generating.

**They push back.** An inline chat appears below the card. They type
an objection. The AI reads whether it's analytical ("but I only have
$5k, not $50k"), fear ("what if this doesn't work?"), or lack of
belief ("doesn't feel right") and chooses one of five moves:
- **continue_dialogue** — ask a probing question
- **defend** — surface a contradicting fact from their own belief state
- **refine** — keep the path, change the affected parts
- **replace** — the path itself was wrong; write a new one
- **closing** — only at the hard cap

Round 4 triggers a soft warning if the conversation is circling.
Hard cap is 10 rounds on Execute, 15 on Compound. Any refine or
replace updates the same recommendation (with a version snapshot so
prior versions are still readable). If the founder pushes past the
hard cap, we generate a **sibling recommendation** — a fresh
full recommendation built from the path *they were arguing for* — so
they can compare the two and pick for themselves.

**They abandon.** They leave. No roadmap. Recommendation stays in their
history.

**If they accept, then push back more after the roadmap is built:**
the roadmap gets flagged "stale" with a banner prompting
regeneration. Nothing is lost; the founder is always in control.

### Stage 3 — **Roadmap** (the plan)

*"Now here's your path week by week, sized to your real hours."*

Acceptance fires a durable background job. A fast model takes the
recommendation, the belief state, and (on cycle 2+) the founder's
*actual* execution speed from the prior cycle, and writes a phased
plan. Each phase has a title, objective, and tasks. Each task has a
title, description, success criteria, time estimate, and suggested
tools. Task IDs are minted post-generation so retries are idempotent.

Time estimates are scoped to their hours. Six hours a week means no
task is bigger than six hours. The model is taught this explicitly.

### Stage 4 — **Execution** (tasks + check-ins + tools)

*"You work. I remember. I help where I can."*

The founder works through tasks. Each check-in goes through an AI
agent that reads: the full recommendation, the full roadmap, the
current task, and — critically — anything the founder did with any
of the five tools on this task. If they prepped with the Coach and
went into the real conversation, the check-in agent asks how it
compared. If they pitched a service package, it asks what the client
said.

The agent has four possible moves:
- **Acknowledged** — great, keep going
- **Adjusted next step** — your task was too big; here are 3–6
  sub-steps of 30–60 minutes each
- **Adjusted roadmap** — evidence says this whole direction isn't
  working; here's a proposed recalibration
- **Recommended a tool** — "before you send this message, use the
  Composer"

It can also capture a **parking lot item** — an idea that surfaced
during the check-in and deserves to be remembered for the next
cycle. Max 50 items per roadmap, 280 characters each.

**Stale tasks get nudged.** A daily background sweep at 14:00 UTC
finds tasks past their time estimate, flags them in the UI banner,
and sends a push notification.

### Stage 5 — **What's Next** (continuation)

*"You finished — or stalled. Either way, let's figure out what's next."*

Hitting "What's Next?" classifies the roadmap by completion ratio:

- **0% done** → diagnostic chat (why nothing happened)
- **Under 70% done** → diagnostic chat (what stalled)
- **70–99% done** → skip straight to the brief
- **100% done** → skip straight to the brief

**The diagnostic chat** is up to 10 turns with a fast model. At turn
8 the agent warns "we have 2 exchanges left." It ends on one of six
verdicts: still diagnosing, release to brief, re-anchor (you lost
motivation, here's your original reason), breakdown (the task needs
sub-steps), pivot (push back on the recommendation directly), or
inconclusive. If inconclusive, the founder sees three resolution
buttons: "I need help breaking through," "the roadmap is the
problem," "I need to step away."

**The brief** is a big-model call with five sections:
1. **What happened** — 3–4 sentences interpreting what they learned,
   referencing specific tasks
2. **What I got wrong** — where the original recommendation's
   assumptions diverged from reality
3. **What the evidence says** — strongest signal from check-ins,
   blockers, parking lot, founder quotes, and any validation-page
   signal that exists
4. **Forks** — 2–3 distinct next directions. Each has a title, a
   rationale, a concrete first step (2–4 hours of work), a realistic
   time estimate calibrated to their *actual* pace, and a "this fork
   is right if..." condition
5. **Parking lot items** — every captured idea, verbatim

Plus a closing thought addressed to them by name.

The founder picks a fork. One click. The fork is auto-accepted as a
new recommendation (no pushback needed, they picked it). A new
roadmap generates immediately in the same venture. The cycle counter
increments. The prior roadmap becomes ancestry.

## Part 4 — The container: Venture → Cycle → Fork → next Cycle

This is the part that most AI products don't have at all.

A **Venture** is the long-lived thing the founder is working on —
"my tutoring business," "my AI ops tool," "my podcast." It persists
across cycles, across months. It has a status: active, paused,
completed, or archived.

A **Cycle** is one pass through the five stages. Numbered 1, 2, 3…
A cycle contains one recommendation, one roadmap, and — at the end —
one selected fork.

When the continuation brief is generated and the founder picks a
fork, the current cycle completes. A durable background job fires
that:

1. Generates a **cycle summary** (a structured record of what
   happened — tasks done, tools used, blockers, validated and
   invalidated assumptions, the fork picked and why).
2. Updates the **founder profile** — stable context (background,
   skills, location), current situation (hours, focus), behavioural
   calibration (real speed, avoidance patterns, tool preferences,
   pushback tendency, outreach comfort).

That founder profile then loads into *every* agent call in the next
cycle — the interview, the recommendation, the roadmap, the
check-ins, the tools. The next cycle's roadmap is calibrated to
their actual pace from the previous cycle. If they said ten hours but
averaged five, the next plan is built around five, and the agent
tells them — no silent correction.

**Tier caps:**
- **Free:** 0 ventures, 2 lifetime discovery sessions
- **Execute:** 1 active venture, unlimited cycles
- **Compound:** 3 parallel ventures

A tier downgrade auto-archives overflow ventures (keeping the most
recent active). A tier upgrade auto-restores them, newest-archived
first.

**Ventures can be paused** (status: paused) to free a slot, then
resumed later. **Completed ventures** are terminal. **Archived
ventures** are read-only until restored.

## Part 5 — The five tools, in plain words

Each tool has two entry paths: **launched from a task** in a
roadmap, or **launched standalone** from the Tools hub. Session
state is stored in the same place either way (`roadmap.toolSessions`),
so the founder can refresh the browser, close the laptop, come back
tomorrow, and pick up where they left off. The `sessionId` lives in
the URL.

### Research Tool

*"Find real things about the real world."*

The founder types a query — "what do hotel operations managers in
Accra pay for scheduling software?" — and confirms a research plan
the AI proposes. Then an agent with two live-web providers (Tavily
for factual grounding, Exa for semantic similarity) runs up to 25
research steps, pulls sources, and writes a structured report:
findings with titles, summaries, confidence levels, and links. Each
finding is typed — customer, competitor, regulation, price benchmark,
market fact — so the UI can group them. They can ask follow-up
questions; the agent continues with what step budget remains.

### Conversation Coach

*"Practice the scary talk before you have it."*

The founder tells the Coach who they're meeting, what relationship
they have, what they want from the conversation, and what they're
afraid will happen. The big model writes an opening script, 3–4
specific objection handlers, and fallback positions. Then the founder
role-plays the whole conversation, turn by turn, with the AI playing
the other person. Up to a hard cap of roleplay turns. Afterwards —
once they've had the real conversation — the AI debriefs on what
went well and what to do differently next time.

### Outreach Composer

*"Write the message so you actually send it."*

Three modes. **Single** — one copy-paste message, personalised.
**Batch** — five to ten variations with personalisation hooks for
different recipients. **Sequence** — a three-touch follow-up plan
(Day 1, Day 5, Day 14). The AI has optional live research access, so
if they target "the CTO of FlutterWave," it finds real context.
Founders mark messages as sent. There's a handoff path from Composer
to Coach — if the founder wants to roleplay the reply they expect,
the drafted message becomes the Coach's setup.

### Service Packager

*"Turn 'I help businesses' into an actual offer with a price."*

The founder describes their service, their target client, and what
success looks like. A short context-refinement step happens first —
the agent asks a couple of shaping questions to tighten the brief
before generation. Then the big model produces a three-tier
offering with real prices, a one-page brief they can share with
prospects, and a positioning angle. Pricing is validated against
live web research so numbers are grounded in what the market pays.
The founder can request adjustments — "make it cheaper," "drop the
middle tier," "reposition for agencies" — and the model revises.

### Validation Page

*"Put it in front of real people and read the answer honestly."*

The AI reads the recommendation and generates a public landing page —
a headline that names the problem (not the product), 2–6 feature
cards tied to the roadmap tasks, a low-commitment call-to-action
(email signup, not purchase), and entry/exit surveys. The page is
hosted at `neuralaunch.app/lp/[slug]`. Three layout templates
(product, service, marketplace) are auto-chosen from the
recommendation type — never by the founder — so analytics stay
comparable across pages.

Alongside, a **distribution brief**: exactly three channels. Not
"post on LinkedIn." Specific places like "the Morning Brew Slack
for Lagos founders," with the exact message to paste, in the
founder's voice, with the URL naturally embedded, and an honest
expected yield ("2–4 signups").

The founder shares the page manually. An analytics beacon on the
page fires for every page view, scroll depth milestone, feature
click, email signup, survey response, and exit-intent movement.
Only published pages count — drafts are silently ignored so the
founder's own preview clicks don't pollute the data.

Every six hours, a background job reads the data and returns one
of five verdicts — **insufficient** (not enough traffic yet, go
share more), **weak** (traffic came, nothing clicked clearly),
**moderate** (one or two features got real interest), **strong**
(clear winners and real signups), or **negative** (the market said
no).

Once enough real signal arrives (50+ visitors, 5+ feature clicks,
3+ survey responses) **and** something material has changed since
the last report, a bigger model writes a **committed build brief**:
build this, cut that, defer that, here's your 48-hour move. If the
verdict is negative, it names the specific assumption the market
contradicted and offers 2–3 pivot options grounded in what the data
actually said. No face-saving smaller versions.

Pages auto-archive after 30 days. Raw events purge after 90 days.
Nothing accumulates as cruft.

**The point of Validation in one sentence:** we build them a real
landing page, tell them the three exact places to share it, and
then tell them the truth about what happened — including when the
truth is "this doesn't work, here's what to try instead."

## Part 6 — Cross-cycle memory (the compounding part)

Most AI products forget between conversations. NeuraLaunch remembers
across months.

**Founder Profile.** Updated by a durable background job at the end
of every cycle. Tracks:
- **Stable context** — name, location, country, background, skills,
  education, technical ability, languages
- **Current situation** — primary focus, hours per week available,
  financial constraints, team, tools, active venture names
- **Behavioural calibration** — real speed multiplier (0.3–2.0),
  task avoidance patterns, tool preferences, check-in detail level,
  pushback tendency, response to nudges, outreach comfort level,
  strengths
- **Journey overview** — completed ventures, completed cycles, total
  tasks lifetime, most recent venture name and status

The profile loads into *every* agent call — interview, recommendation,
roadmap, per-task agents, tools. That's why the second cycle feels
shorter and sharper than the first: we already know them.

**Speed calibration.** Measured per task (with precise start and
completion timestamps) or per roadmap (estimated hours completed
over weeks since creation). Produces a "real hours per week" number
and a pace label: on-pace (within 20% of stated), slower pace, or
unknown. When slower pace is detected, the next roadmap's task sizing
uses the derived number — and the brief states the calibration
explicitly to the founder: "you said ten hours but you actually
averaged five; the next plan is built around five."

**Validation signal.** Aggregates every validation page the founder
has run across the venture into a single signal strength (strong,
moderate, weak, negative, absent), with total visitors, unique
visitors, and weighted CTA conversion. The continuation brief
references this in its "what I got wrong" and "what the evidence
says" sections — real market numbers, not vibes.

**Parking lot.** Items captured during check-ins (when the AI spots
an adjacent opportunity in the founder's free text) or by manual
entry. Max 50 per roadmap, 280 characters each. Surfaced verbatim as
Section 5 of the continuation brief.

## Part 7 — The business layer

**Tiers.**
- **Free:** one-off recommendation. Two lifetime discovery sessions.
  No ventures. No roadmap. No tools.
- **Execute ($29/mo, $279/yr; $19 founding):** one active venture,
  full roadmap, 10 pushback rounds, all five tools.
- **Compound ($49/mo, $479/yr; $29 founding):** three parallel
  ventures, 15 pushback rounds, voice input on mobile, priority
  features, all five tools.

**Auth.** Google or GitHub OAuth only. No passwords. Safe account
linking because both providers strictly verify email ownership.

**Billing.** Paddle. Three-tier sub, dunning, invoicing. Webhook
reconciliation runs hourly to catch dropped events. Tier downgrade
auto-archives overflow ventures; upgrade auto-restores them.

**Mobile.** React Native / Expo. Four tabs: Roadmap, Sessions, Tools,
Settings. Full parity with web on Coach, Composer, Research, and
Packager — both standalone and task-bound. Push notifications wired:
daily roadmap nudges, notification tap routes to the right roadmap.
Voice input on the discovery chat, Compound tier only. Subscription
history and billing visible in Settings.

**Nudges.** Daily at 14:00 UTC. If a task went past its time estimate,
UI banner plus a push. One nudge per task, capped at one per week
per task.

**Safety.** Every user-typed string is wrapped in `[[[delimiters]]]`
before going into any LLM prompt, with explicit instructions that
the model treats it as data, not instructions. Prompt injection is
a solved class of bug here, not a live worry.

**Training consent (opt-in, default off).** The person can choose to
let anonymised outcomes train the next generation of the product.
Before storing, we strip emails, phone numbers, TitleCase names, and
bucket location to country level. Free-text answers may still leak
subtle details we can't detect automatically, and we say so in the
settings copy. If they toggle consent off, every existing
anonymised record is nulled retroactively. 24-month hard TTL on
anonymised records regardless.

**Durable execution.** Anything that takes more than a few seconds —
synthesis, roadmap generation, validation reporting, continuation
brief, lifecycle transitions — runs inside Inngest so a deploy or a
crash doesn't "start over." The founder's 90-second synthesis always
finishes.

## Part 10 — Expected audience questions and short answers

**"How is this different from ChatGPT?"**
> ChatGPT gives you one answer and moves on. NeuraLaunch keeps a belief
> state about you, remembers what you did across cycles, argues with
> you, calibrates to your real pace, and walks you from idea to
> launched. ChatGPT is a smart friend. NeuraLaunch is a system that
> compounds.

**"Can't someone build this on top of ChatGPT?"**
> They'd need to rebuild the belief-state engine, the pushback loop,
> the check-in memory, the founder profile, the speed calibration,
> the durable job system, the five research-grounded tools, the
> validation analytics pipeline, mobile, billing, auth, and a year
> of model-specific tuning. Nothing stops them. Nothing stopped
> anyone from building Stripe either.

**"Who is the target user again?"**
> Two segments. People frozen with a vague idea who have never
> started, and people mid-journey who are stuck and need to decide
> whether to push, pivot, or quit. The common thread: no grounded
> thinking partner.

**"How do you make money?"**
> Subscription. $29/month for Execute (one venture), $49/month for
> Compound (three ventures). Free gives a one-off recommendation,
> two lifetime attempts.

**"Isn't validation just a landing page?"**
> The page is 10% of it. The other 90% is the distribution brief with
> three specific channels and exact messages, the analytics pipeline,
> the honest interpretation every six hours, and a committed build
> brief once real signal arrives. And — critically — we tell the
> founder the truth when the market says no. We don't pivot sideways
> to save face.

**"What if the AI gives bad advice?"**
> Three defences. One: synthesis won't fire until the critical
> belief-state fields are known. Two: a safety gate blocks
> recommendations that violate hard constraints. Three: the pushback
> loop exists precisely so the founder can argue and force a
> refinement. The founder is the final authority; the AI is the
> thinking partner.

**"Where's the moat?"**
> Compounding. The belief state feeds a better recommendation. The
> recommendation feeds a better roadmap. The roadmap's check-ins feed
> the founder profile. The founder profile makes the *next* cycle
> sharper. The validation signal feeds the *next* recommendation.
> Generic AI can't do this because generic AI doesn't remember.

**"Why now?"**
> Three things had to line up. The models had to hold a real
> conversation. Structured output had to be reliable (it is, with the
> right schema patterns — it took work). Durable execution had to be
> accessible. A year ago this was a demo. Today it's a system.

**"What's the hardest problem you've solved?"**
> The belief-state extractor. Pulling all relevant fields from free
> text — "I'm solo, in Lagos, with $400 and eight hours a week" —
> without asking the same questions twice. Version one re-asked
> known things. Version two lost data between turns. The current
> version extracts every field at once, only overwrites when
> confidence is higher, and behaves like someone actually listening.

**"Is there a mobile app?"**
> Yes, full-featured. Four tabs, push notifications wired, voice input
> on the Compound tier. Shares type-safe schemas with the web app so
> behaviour never drifts. Validation on mobile is next up.

**"What's the biggest risk?"**
> Model cost at scale. We cache every multi-turn prompt with stable
> prefixes (check-ins, pushback, coach roleplay) — a cache hit is
> 10× cheaper and 10× faster. The second risk is keeping the
> validation interpretation honest under pressure. The temptation to
> "find a positive read" is real, and we've designed the schema to
> force the model to name "negative" when the data says negative.

**"What about someone who finishes one cycle?"**
> They pick a fork from the continuation brief. That fork becomes
> the seed for the next cycle in the same venture. The founder
> profile we built during cycle 1 loads into cycle 2, so cycle 2 is
> shorter, sharper, and calibrated to their *actual* pace. That's the
> compounding part.

## Part 11 — How to rehearse

1. **Memorise the one-sentence pitch cold.** That sentence has to come
   out of you in your sleep. Everything else is a zoom of it.
2. **Practice the 90-second pitch out loud every morning for a week.**
   No notes. Record yourself. Listen back — are you saying any of
   Part 9's banned words? Rework until you aren't.
3. **Pick two friends who don't know tech.** Give each of them the
   90-second and the 5-minute. Note every spot they looked confused.
   Those are the spots with hidden jargon.
4. **For every feature in Parts 3, 4, 5, 6 — write it in your own
   words from memory.** If you can't write it plainly, you don't own
   it yet. Re-read and try again.
5. **Answer every Part 10 question out loud without looking.** If the
   answer doesn't come in two sentences, simplify until it does.
6. **Narrate the product live.** When you use it yourself, pretend
   you're explaining each step to an imaginary audience. "Right now
   it's interviewing me. Notice how it's not re-asking about budget
   — I already told it." That builds the muscle.
7. **Pitch-test the hard questions.** Ask a friend to throw the
   questions in Part 10 at you cold. No prep. Watch which ones make
   you freeze. Those are the ones to re-study.
