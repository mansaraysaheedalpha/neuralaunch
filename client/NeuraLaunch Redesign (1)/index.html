You asked the right question. The product you've built is rare — a multi-cycle execution engine with prompt-cached agents, durable workers, and a real point of view. The UI does not yet carry that weight. This memo sets out what's working, what isn't, and the two directions I'd take it.

§ 01 The verdict

The current design is competent SaaS. The product is institutional. The gap between the two is where premium lives — and it is yours to close.

Yes — a lot can be done. Not by adding things. By removing things, committing to one voice, and letting the words do the work. The product's biggest asset right now is the writing in your vision document. The UI should get out of its way and let it ring.

§ 02 Findings
Finding 01 · Composition

The landing is nine sections. It should be five.

Hero, Problem, How It Works, One Recommendation, Tools, Differentiation, Stays-With-You, Stories, Pricing, Final CTA — each one re-explaining the others.

Top-tier products trust a single idea per scroll. Linear has six sections. Vercel has five. McKinsey's homepage has four. Density is not the enemy of premium — repetition is.

The move Collapse to: Stand · Cycle · Surface · Price. Kill the differentiation table — the product's voice already does that work. Move Stories into Cycle.

Finding 02 · Mock soup

Too many tiny mocks. Not enough product.

InterviewMock, RoadmapMock, ContinuationBriefMock, ToolsRowMock, four BeatMocks, ProblemSpotlight, RecommendationPreviewMock — each one well-intentioned, all of them together fighting for the eye.

The premium move is one or two real product surfaces, hero-sized, presented like Apple presents a phone. The product is good enough to be photographed.

The move One full-bleed discovery transcript on the hero. One real roadmap card in section three. Everything else is words.

Eight illustrations across the page, none big enough to be believed. A single screenshot at full width would land harder than all of them combined.

Finding 03 · Voice and surface

The writing is institutional. The UI is generic shadcn.

Your vision document reads like a McKinsey memo. The pages it produces read like every Next.js app shipped this year: max-w-2xl rounded-xl border bg-card with lucide icons in tinted squares.

You are inviting people to be told the truth. The container should look like a memo — typographically considered, not card-y.

The move Editorial type (serif headlines or one statement sans), hairline rules instead of cards, monospace labels for metadata, and at most one accent colour. The shadcn card is the enemy of premium.

Finding 04 · Two accents

Blue says fintech. Gold says luxury.
You have both.

Electric blue + warm gold reads as two products fighting for ownership of the brand. Stripe is one colour. Linear is one colour. Anthropic is one warm. McKinsey is navy. DeepMind is near-black. They commit.

The gold is doing more work than it earns; it pulls toward "premium SaaS" instead of "world-class strategic partner."

The move Pick a single accent and use it like punctuation. Hold the rest to neutrals.

Finding 05 · /discovery

The Discovery surface looks like a chatbot. It is not.

You flagged this yourself, and you are right. Discovery is the single most defining moment of the product — the interview that earns the right to commit to one path. Right now it is rendered as a generic message thread inside a generic chat window.

When the product asks for context before conclusion, the surface should signal this is being read carefully — not type into the box like every other AI app.

The move A two-column transcript: the live question on the left like an interview prompt, the founder's answers as recorded testimony on the right. A visible belief-state panel that fills in as you speak. Treat it like a clinical intake, not a chat.
What's the situation you're trying to change?
I run a small clinic. Growth has stalled and I'm not sure if it's pricing or positioning…
Got it. How many hours a week can you actually give this?
Type your reply ↑

This is the visual grammar of every consumer chatbot. Your product's first impression should feel closer to a session transcript than a conversation with a friend.
